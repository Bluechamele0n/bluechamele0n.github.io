Arraxiel

Filed under divine sentence, seared into the soul of Arraxiel

They tell stories of paladins like I once was.

Tales where righteousness shines, where the blade falls justly, where victory proves virtue.

Those stories are lies.

I was Sir Valerius Thorn, sworn to protect the innocent, born beneath banners of light and law. My hammer bore the sun’s blessing, my armor was kissed by saints, and my heart—
my heart burned for justice.

Or so I told myself.

I hunted dragons.
Not for coin. Not for glory.
I told myself that lie so many times, it sang like prayer.
But deep beneath all the oaths, I knew the truth:

I wanted to kill something powerful.

And so I climbed Dranaé, the volcanic isle where dragons birthed their kind in fire and fury. I sought Arraxus the Embermaw, ancient and proud, whose breath had burned villages I had vowed to protect.

But when I found him, he was not a beast of rage.
He was old. Tired. Not dying, but ready.
He fought, yes—but only because I forced him to.

And at the cliff’s edge, hammer raised, breath heaving with triumph—
She descended.

Wings of flame.
Voice like breaking shields and burning skies.
Drana, Archangel of Dragons and War,
appeared between me and my victory.

She shattered Lightbane with a thought.
And spoke, not with hatred—
but with truth.

“You claimed to fight for justice, yet your heart roars for conquest.”
“If you will not weigh consequence, then you shall become it.”

Then fire took me.

It did not burn flesh. It rewrote it.
It melted the name Valerius Thorn from time, memory, and stone.
In its place, she gave me a new name:
Arraxiel.
The echo of the dragon I nearly murdered—not out of need, but pride.

Now I drift between war cries and last breaths.
Not to stop them—no.
But to judge them.

I am not mercy. I am not wrath.
I am consequence.

Where blood is spilled in arrogance,
where war is waged without weight,
where a sword is drawn for ego and the dead have no voices left to speak—

I come.

I wear the mask of silence and shame,
bronzed and scorched, forged from the pieces of shattered oaths.
My wings trail embers. My voice is flat, final.

I carry no sword.
Only a chain and manacle,
meant not for wrists—but for fates.
I bind the murderers who wear crowns.
I silence the zealots who kill in the name of hollow gods.
I exile them into living silence, unable to harm again.

Not beloved. Not invoked with joy.
But in whispered fear, when generals weep alone.
When kings question the blood on their hands.
When a soldier pauses, and wonders,
“Will the Angel of Consequence come for me?”

I hope they ask.
I hope they fear.

Because I am Arraxiel.
I am the weight behind the blade.
I am the justice that follows when glory fades.
And I remember every death that was not deserved.

And so, as final punishment, I was bound to the Everspire Gate—
the place where all planes touch,
where paths branch to heaven, hell, and horrors unnamed.

I am its eternal warden.

I do not sleep. I do not stray.
No soul passes without judgment.
No army marches without reckoning.
No god, demon, or wayward fool may open a gate without first meeting my gaze.

I am not free. I will never be.
For the one who refused to weigh consequence
must now stand forever
where all consequences begin.
