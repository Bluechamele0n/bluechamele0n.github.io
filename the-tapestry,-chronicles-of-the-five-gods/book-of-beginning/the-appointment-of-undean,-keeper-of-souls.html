The Appointment of Undean, Keeper of Souls

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

In the early days of Fiondell, when the world was still young, the gods looked upon the cycle of life and death with a discerning gaze. They saw the delicate balance that bound the living to the dead, a balance that must never be disrupted, for the entire world would crumble if it were. Yet, as always, the actions of mortals created ripples, some of which marred the natural order. Souls, once destined for the Beyond, lingered too long in the mortal realm. Others, twisted by the dark magics of mortals, fell into states that should not have been. These souls were neither dead nor living, but something in between, and this, too, troubled the gods.

I recall that moment in the heavens as if it were yesterday, though my memories span eons. The gods gathered, their presence vast and unshakable, yet there was a tension in the air. They spoke in whispers, as if the very fabric of time itself held its breath.

Death, ever the serene and solemn guide of the departed, stood in silence. Her beauty was not the beauty of the living, but the quiet elegance of finality. I remember her voice, soft and melodic, yet filled with a certainty that only the passage of time could impart.

"The souls I guide, I carry with great care. But some remain, tethered to the mortal world. Others, tainted by mortal hands, begin to decay, like fruit left too long in the sun. This is no natural state."

The gods did not speak immediately, but I knew that they understood. The task was too great for any one of them, for life and death were not meant to be meddled with lightly. And so, they turned to Undean, whose presence had always been... unusual.

Undean’s arrival was unlike any other. Her form, a specter caught between realms, flickered like a fading ember. The pallor of her skin resembled the cold stone of a forgotten tomb, and her eyes glowed faintly—faint, like the lanterns of the dead that flicker in the darkness. But it was not just her appearance that marked her as different. There was something about the way she moved, as though she existed both here and not here at all. It was as if her very presence whispered of boundaries crossed and forgotten places.

I stood by, as I always do, watching and remembering. The gods—Fionil, Onigod, and Trill—approached Undean one by one, as though offering pieces of their will for her to carry.

Fionil, the goddess who shaped time itself, stepped forward first. Her voice was both calm and unyielding, as if she was the clock that turned the cosmos.

"Undean, the boundary between life and death is sacred. You must not allow it to be corrupted. The souls must be guided with care, but they must not be bound to the world of the living. You will ensure they leave, untainted by the hands of mortals."

I remember how Undean bowed. Her head lowered, her shoulders squared, and she whispered,

"It is a heavy task, my lady, but one I will bear. No soul shall linger where it does not belong."

Her words were soft, yet there was a finality in them, a certainty that resonated deep within me.

Then Onigod, the goddess of Arcane and Monsters, stepped forward.

"The living have already begun their meddling,"

she said, her voice a mixture of quiet frustration and controlled power.

"They seek to control the dead, to use them as puppets in their dark games. You, Undean, shall be their undoing. When the living reach into the world of the dead, you will stand as the wall they cannot breach. You will be the end to their perversion."

Undean nodded, her eyes narrowing slightly.

"I will ensure that no soul is enslaved, no matter the strength of mortal magic."

Her resolve was clear, and for the first time, I saw the fire that burned within her—a fire not of destruction, but of preservation.

Finally, Trill, the god of Life and Dragons, who had always viewed life as a precious, fleeting gift, spoke. His voice was filled with a warmth that could only be found in the deep places of the world.

"Undean, souls are the very essence of life. Without them, there is nothing. Protect them from corruption, and guide those who are lost back to the cycle. You and Death will work as one, for it is through your union that the balance will remain."

I saw Undean’s eyes soften, just for a moment, as she nodded in understanding.

"I will guide them, Trill. The lost shall find their way, and the corrupt shall be cast aside."

And so, it was decided. The gods bestowed upon Undean two sacred gifts—tools to aid her in her task, tools that would forever tie her to the delicate web of life and death.

Fionil handed her the Lantern of Rest, a lantern that held the pale blue flame of forgotten memories.

"With this light, you will reveal the truth to wandering souls. The lost shall find their way, and the corrupt will be undone. Its glow will banish the shadows that trap the dead."

Then Onigod gave her the Blade of Severance, a weapon forged from the very fabric of the void between life and death.

"This blade will cut the bonds that tether souls to the mortal realm. Use it wisely, for it bears the weight of finality."

Her voice was both warning and promise.

Finally, Death herself stepped forward, offering Undean a hand.

"Together, we will ensure that no soul strays too far, that no dark force will claim them. We are bound by purpose, Undean. I will guide them; you will shield them."

Undean took Death's hand with a quiet strength, her voice a whisper carried on the wind.

"Together, we will ensure that the dead find peace and the living honor the cycle."

The work began. Undean descended to the mortal world, a being of light and shadow, of life and death. To the living, she was a ghostly figure, a reminder of the finality that awaited them all. Her presence brought fear, yes—but also respect. She became a specter of judgment, a reminder that death was not to be taken lightly.

But to the dead, she was something else entirely. She was a guide, a gentle shepherd who led them away from the torment of the living. Her lantern illuminated the path to peace, her voice soft but unyielding. She spoke to the lost, to those who clung to the mortal realm with fear or anger.

"You cannot remain here,"

she would say.

"The living cannot give you what you seek. Let go, and find peace in the Beyond."

Her work was tireless. Necromancers who sought to raise the dead found their efforts shattered by the swing of Undean's Blade of Severance. Dark mages who attempted to trap souls in their own twisted machinations found their spells undone by the flicker of the Lantern of Rest. And whenever a soul was trapped, refusing to pass into the Beyond, Undean was there, a quiet, comforting presence in the dark.

It was not an easy task, this one she had taken. But it was necessary. The balance of life and death had been restored, and though the living would always fear what awaited them, they could not deny the harmony Undean brought to the world.

I remember the gods watching from their realms, their hearts filled with pride. Death and Undean, together, had become the guardians of the cycle. Their work was not glamorous, nor was it easy. But it was essential, for without them, the world would have fallen into chaos.

And so, Undean’s light continues to shine. She remains a symbol of both warning and comfort. To those who would defile the dead, she is the implacable force that will never relent. But to the grieving and the lost, she is the guiding hand that ensures the dead find rest, and the living remember the sanctity of the cycle.

As I record these events, I am reminded of the delicate balance that sustains us all. Life, death, and the space between. It is a thread that must not be severed, for it binds us all. And in this, Undean’s work will never end.
