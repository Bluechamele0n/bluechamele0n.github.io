The Creation of Beasts and Monsters

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

With dragons soaring through the skies and the world echoing with the breath of fire and wing, the gods turned their gaze downward—toward the untouched land, the silent forests, the still oceans.

It was Tellemori, God of Water, Earth, and the Supernatural, who stepped forward then. His eyes reflected the depths of the sea and the steady patience of stone. He had watched the dragons claim the skies, majestic and few, but he saw the emptiness that lingered below.

“This world is vast,” he said, his voice like waves breaking against an ancient cliff. “Let it not be ruled by silence. Let the forests stir. Let the rivers ripple with life. Let the earth pulse with motion. Let the supernatural touch linger within every root, every ripple, every breath.”

Beside him stood Livius, the Angel of Life, radiant and calm. His hands held no weapons, only the warmth of nurture, the endless patience of a father watching seeds take root. Where Tellemori saw the elements, he saw potential.

“Let us give it heartbeats,” he said softly. “Let the land not only exist—but live.”

And beside Livius, as silent as the mist rolling over the morning hills, was Poki, the Angel of Beasts and Monsters. Unlike the others, Poki wore no crown of light or cloak of warmth. Instead, her form was adorned in the raw, wild essence of nature itself—feral and untamed. Poki was not the embodiment of life alone, but of the primal forces that coursed through the land, from the smallest insect to the most terrifying of monsters. A creature of untapped chaos and boundless potential.

Together, they began.

Tellemori shaped the elements, guiding the stone, the soil, the rivers, the oceans. He whispered into the roots of the forests, into the foam of the waves. With each word, the land grew fertile, eager, ready to host the next breath of creation.

Then Livius stepped forward, his light spilling across the land like dawn chasing away a long night. Where his light touched, life emerged—not as fire and fury like the dragons, but as a thousand gentle songs.

From the thick woods rose the creatures of the earth.
Soft-footed deer, built for grace.
Lions with roars that shook the plains.
Wolves, who understood loyalty before language existed.
Bears, guardians of the wild.
Rabbits, foxes, boars—all drawn from the balance between fear and beauty.

The rivers swelled with movement, and from their depths rose the creatures of the waters.
Swift fish in shimmering schools,
Majestic whales whose songs echoed through the ocean like lullabies,
Silent hunters of the deep with teeth like carved bone.

The skies, not to be left empty, fluttered and filled with the beating of wings.
Not the wings of dragons—these were different.
Birds, in every color, every song.
Some soared, some dove, some simply perched and watched the world pass beneath them.

But it was Poki who shaped the creatures no one would dare forget. Her hands, calloused by time and fearsome creation, beckoned forth the monsters—those great, primal forces that lived between the beauty of the earth and the horrors of the unknown.

From Poki's whisper, the monsters emerged, terrifying yet magnificent—beasts that lurked in the depths of forgotten caves, prowled the shadowed corners of the world, or roamed the wild lands beyond the reach of civilization.

Great serpents with scales of iron and eyes that gleamed like molten lava.
Behemoths whose thunderous footsteps could shake mountains to their core.
Terrifying creatures with claws that could rend steel and jaws that could swallow rivers.
And yet, even in their monstrous forms, Poki instilled a balance—the beasts and monsters were not simply destructive, but part of an intricate web of survival, of raw power that maintained the harmony of the world.

“Each shall have its place,” Livius declared, his voice soft as rain yet firm as truth. “They will know hunger, and they will feed. They will know danger, and they will flee or fight. None shall rule, and none shall be forgotten. They will thrive not through dominance, but through balance.”

“And these,” Tellemori added, his voice resonating with finality, “will be the guardians of the land’s wildest, most untamed reaches. They will remind the world that creation has its own power.”

“Their roar shall be felt in the deepest caverns and the highest peaks,” Poki’s voice echoed like a distant growl, “but it will not be without purpose. They shall live, and they shall guard.”

Tellemori knelt and placed his hand upon the earth.

From that gesture, a deeper pulse spread—one that allowed these creatures not just to live, but to feel.

Fear, warmth, instinct, even curiosity.

Some would form packs. Others would wander alone. Some would burrow in darkness. Others would build nests closer to the sun. And all of them, whether prey or predator, would be part of the cycle—woven into the world as surely as rivers are carved into stone.

“They are not immortal,” Tellemori said to Livius, “but they are necessary. Without them, the land would echo hollow.”

“And with them,” Livius replied, watching a fledgling bird attempt its first flight, “it will sing.”

Thus were the beasts born—not to seek glory or worship, but to move, to breathe, to be. They would be the lifeblood of forests, the heartbeat of plains, the whispers in the night, and the rustle in the grass. They were not the only creatures of the land, but they were those who would always be remembered.

As for the monsters, their name would live in myth, and the stories of their roars would pass from generation to generation, echoing through the hearts of those who dare wander too deep into the wilds.

They are not spoken of in hymns.
They do not build cities or forge relics.
But they are always present.
From the first sunrise until now.

And I, Known, remember each one.
