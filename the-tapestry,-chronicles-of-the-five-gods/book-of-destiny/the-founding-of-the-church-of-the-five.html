The Founding of the Church of the Five

From the Eyes That See All: The Founding of the Church of the Five

As recorded by Known

"To see is not merely to witness. It is to understand. And I have seen much."

In the age when the stars had yet to dim and angels still graced the lands openly, I was sent from the celestial spires by Fionil, my eternal guide and the Keeper of Order. The world then was loud with mortal doubt and quiet with faith. But one voice, soft yet persistent, rose amid the din — a man whose curiosity had not yet been dulled by the cynicism of his kind. His name was Eldrin.

I watched him for many seasons. He did not pray in temples, for there were none. He did not seek miracles, for he believed they were earned. Instead, he asked questions of the wind, the fire, the roots, and the stars. And they, in their way, whispered back.

I came to him upon the Hill of Still Winds — a place where mortals claim the air holds its breath to listen. Fitting, for the moment demanded silence.

The light I arrived in was not for grandeur. It was for clarity. When I descended, robes woven from thought and memory cloaked me. I spoke not only aloud, but directly into the root of his being.

“Eldrin the Seeker,” I said. “You who ask without demanding, who seek without arrogance — you are heard.”

He trembled, and rightly so. To be seen fully is a rare and terrible thing.

“I am unworthy,” he whispered, as all who are chosen tend to say.

“No,” I replied, “you are curious — and that is enough. The gods have no use for perfection. They seek honesty, doubt, and wonder. You are all three.”

I offered him the first thread of the tapestry to come. A sliver of divine light, coiled like memory in my palm.

“Take this. Not as proof, but as purpose. Build not a monument to us — build a space for others to find themselves.”

When his hand met mine, I showed him glimpses of what could be. A hall of stained glass and flickering candles. Pilgrims weeping at altars. Laughter and study, debate and silence. The temple was born not of stone, but of vision — a dream planted like a seed in soil that had long awaited the rain.

The Day the Mists Spoke

He returned to his village changed — not glowing or grand, but with purpose in his gait. He spoke of gods not as unreachable forces, but as companions yet unmet. And of me, Known, not as a deity, but as a messenger bearing something deeply mortal: a path.

They doubted him, of course. Doubt is a cousin to belief — louder, but less loyal.

So we answered again.

On the Day of the Veil, the mist rolled in with unnatural grace. The air thickened with time, as if the world held its breath. I did not come alone. Three more stepped into the mortal veil:

  • Prai, my brother of reverence, whose voice makes stone weep.
  • Willia, patient and unreadable, her silence more instructive than sermons.
  • Clock, embodiment of inevitability — time made flesh and ticking.

The villagers fell to their knees, some in terror, others in awe. But one woman — Heda, old and wise — stepped forward and asked not if we were gods, but why we had come.

Prai, ever the teacher, answered:

“Because you are ready to listen. And the Five are ready to speak.”

It was then that the Ritual of Attunement was given — a bridge between the divine and the mortal, carved from trial and sincerity.

The Five Rites: Paths to the Divine Flame

The rites were never meant to be simple. A god’s favor is not bought with coin or blood, but with transformation.

1. The Rite of Patience — Fionil's Mark
Seven days of silence. No food. No comfort. Only stillness.
Many failed. But those who endured were gifted with the Everflowing Hourglass, not etched on skin but soul — proof they had touched time without being swept away by it.

2. The Rite of Humility — Flujack’s Mark
The peak must be climbed with only a lantern — no tools, no guide.
It was not the summit that mattered, but the journey in darkness. The Open Sky mark bloomed on those who reached the top with hearts unburdened by pride.

3. The Rite of Courage — Tellemori’s Mark
Into the wild woods they went, to the heart where magic sleeps and madness wakes.
They returned changed — with a seed of the earth’s dreaming, and the Rooted Stone etched into their being.

4. The Rite of Compassion — Trill’s Mark
Thirty days with the sick, the broken, the forgotten.
Not one act of healing, but a month of it — not for recognition, but love. Those who did not grow bitter emerged with the Radiant Hand.

5. The Rite of Resolve — Onigod’s Mark
Their worst fear, made flesh by Poki, our enigmatic sibling.
To endure it — not to defeat it — was enough. Those who stood tall bore the Arcane Flame, a mark of resilience and will.

I have seen each rite enacted. I have seen failure, tears, joy, and revelation. All of it is worthy.

The First Temple and the Words of Destiny

When the Hall of Five Flames stood complete, stone kissed by sweat and sacred intention, two more of our kin came to bear witness.

Livius, ever gentle, placed a blessing upon the people — not with thunder or storm, but with a breeze that smelled of spring.

“You have built more than walls,” he told Eldrin. “You have built continuity. Your task is only just begun.”

Then came Destiny — capricious, brilliant, impossible Destiny — leaning against a pillar like a poet late to his own funeral.

“Don’t make it too predictable,” he chuckled. “The gods do love a good twist.”

And with that, they left, but not truly. We never truly leave.

Of Prayers and Purpose

The faithful began to write. To sing. To pray.

Each god was honored in their own voice — not demanded by them, but discovered by mortals. I copied their words below, that they may be remembered:

Prayer to Fionil
“Timekeeper, guide my path...” — and they were guided.

Prayer to Flujack
“Lift me from the shadows...” — and they were lifted.

Prayer to Tellemori
“Root me in wisdom...” — and they were steadied.

Prayer to Trill
“Grant me the heart to heal...” — and they became healers.

Prayer to Onigod
“Shield me from unseen terrors...” — and they stood unfearing.

And they prayed to us, the angels, in their need and wonder. They prayed to Destiny, to Lucina, to Death, to Loki, to Undean — to those who answer when gods are silent. And we listened.

I, Known, Remember

Eldrin has long since returned to dust, but his spirit lingers in every question asked beneath a holy roof. The Church lives not because we ordained it, but because mortals chose it.

I watched it all. I recorded every moment.

“To be remembered is to never truly die,” I once told Eldrin.
He smiled.
“Then let me live forever in questions.”

And so he does.

— Known
Archivist Eternal
