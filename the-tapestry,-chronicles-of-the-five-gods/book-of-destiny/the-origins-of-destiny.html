The Origins of Destiny

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

Ah yes, Destiny... I remember the day of his making with perfect clarity. How could I forget, when the very fabric of existence shifted to welcome him? It was a rare thing, you see, for an angel to be born after the dawn of the world. But on that day, the great loom of time itself paused.

Fionil, Goddess of Time, Space, Mind, and Order, sat upon the eternal spire where all threads of fate gather—her Tapestry. She had grown... not weary, no, that would be too simple a word. Curious. Yes, Fionil, the one who weaves all futures, grew curious of the unknown.

"I wish to observe without knowing. To watch a path I myself have not laid."

She summoned us, her angels—Clock, Willia, Filla, and myself. We stood beneath the loom, threads of reality humming around us.

Clock, ever punctual, bowed.

"I would gladly take the role, but time itself is mine to maintain. Without me, it falters."

"Indeed, Clock. Your burden is great, and none can bear it but you," Fionil replied, her tone ever-serene.

Willia spoke next, her voice trembling with honesty.

"I would do it if I could, but where free will thrives, I must not tread. To shape fate is to unmake choice."

"Wise and true, Willia. I would not break what you are," Fionil answered with a nod.

Filla, guardian of emotions, crossed her arms.

"My feelings cloud judgment. I fear I would mold destiny to comfort or to vengeance."

"And so you shall not carry it."

Finally, I stepped forward.

"I am Knowledge. I would want the story to amuse me—to entertain. I would meddle for curiosity's sake. No, I cannot hold this role. But may I offer a thought? Make a fifth. Let there be an Angel of Fate. One untouched by past paths, one born to weave anew."

Fionil's eyes lit with sudden purpose.

"Your honesty is rare even among angels, Known. Let us create him together."

In the blink of a cosmic heartbeat, we were transported to the inner threads of the Tapestry, in a domain Fionil called The Fabric. She flicked her fingers, and from that ripple of will, an angel emerged.

He wore a long purple robe with fine silver trim, a tall feathered hat set jauntily on his head. His cane gleamed like polished obsidian, and in his hand he held a silver mask—not yet worn. His long brown hair fell like waterfalls down his back, and his wings shimmered dark grey with a faint violet glow.

"Ah... Hello, my lady. I believe I shall name myself Destiny, if I may," he said with a smirk that teetered between reverence and mischief. "Curious... I seem to know all that could be, and all that has been. Why is that? And hello, dear siblings. Thank you for having me. So kind."

"Oh, my son... Destiny," Fionil spoke, voice rich with certainty. "You are the Angel of Fate. You shall guide all paths, not by command, but by touch. Subtle. Soft. You shall not force but direct. This domain is yours."

Just then, the other gods appeared: Trill, Flujack, and Tellemori. They studied Destiny, their eyes curious but wary.

But Onigod, Goddess of the Arcane, stepped forward without hesitation.

"You must be the newborn. Hells, it's been forever since we've had a new angel. LUCINA! HOW LONG HAS IT BEEN?!"

Lucina winced.

"Five hundred billion years. And you don’t need to shout, I’m beside you."

She turned to Destiny, eyeing him up and down.

"Come now, newling. Let me show you what is. What has been. Who we are."

Destiny looked to Fionil, who nodded, and then to Trill, who merely shrugged.

And so they walked. Lucina guided him through all that had been made: the constellations of will, the realms of the gods, the mortal worlds below. She introduced him to each of us. I must admit, I found him fascinating. He was like a mirror that bent its reflection, a paradox I could not predict.

"From this day forth, we are best friends. Got it?" Lucina declared with a half-jest, half-dare.

"That sounds delightful. But I must return to Fionil, my beautiful mother."

He bent the threads of space itself and appeared before Fionil, kneeling with dramatic flair.

"Mother. I've seen the stars, the roots, the angels. Shall we begin?"

Fionil paused her weaving.

"Tell me what you desire, my child."

"Not to force paths. Only to guide. To nudge. Let fate weave itself, and let me only mend its tears and point where needed."

She smiled.

"Then do as you will."

In time, Destiny grew powerful. He learned to weave without his loom, to whisper through chance, to pull threads with such precision that none noticed his hand. At first, he needed his chamber — his Loomhall, nested within the Tapestry itself — to perform his craft. But that time did not last. He grew beyond it.

I remember the first moment I saw him bend fate with a gesture alone — not in his domain, but atop the cliffs of Aetheris. A mortal stumbled and should have fallen to his death. But a breeze rose. A vine curled at just the right angle. A second chance bloomed from nothing, and none but I knew he had touched the moment.

Destiny, unlike many of us angels, did not seek admiration. He sought perfection. Quietly, he studied Fionil’s grand design, not to replicate it, but to support it in the way she could not. For though she wove time and space, she could not let go of knowing. Destiny wanted not to know — only to feel when the Tapestry frayed, and mend it without disturbing the weave.

But make no mistake — he was not soft.

When mortals or even minor spirits defied the threads and sought to tear the weave to rewrite their own fates, Destiny did not grant them mercy. He watched. He warned. Then, he acted. A flick of his cane could unravel a thousand futures and resew them in a breath. He was as terrifying as he was graceful.

The gods took notice.

Tellemori, who rarely spoke of change, once said to me,

“Destiny… I once thought the earth eternal and unmoving. But when he speaks, even stone listens.”

Flujack, whose joy often turned to mockery, grew quiet when Destiny entered a room.

“He carries all the stars behind that mask,” he said once. “And I’m not sure he’s laughing.”

Trill, gentle Trill, adored him.

“He balances life like a blade. Never too heavy, never too light. He’s what I wished Time would birth. And she did.”

But it was Onigod, ever wild and curious, who perhaps understood him best.

“He is not one of us. And yet… he is all of us,” she whispered after their first true conversation. “His mask hides not his face — but our limits.”

Even among us angels, he stirred something rare — reverence... and fear.

I have seen Filla turn away when he speaks, not out of disrespect, but from the weight his words carry.
Willia, who guides free will, once told me,

“He makes me question if choice is ever free. And yet, he never steals it — he only... suggests.”

As for me? I am Knowledge. I know most things. But not him. Not all of him. And that is why I walk beside him still.

He does not command loyalty. He inspires it. Through small acts — a shielded mortal here, a redirected tragedy there — he builds unseen monuments of gratitude. He once spent a thousand years rearranging a single bloodline to ensure a war would not happen, and never told the gods he had done it. I only knew because he let me watch.

When Clock, tired and ever-watching, chose to rest his mind within the deeper ticks of time, Fionil did not hesitate.

Destiny rose.

Not in ceremony. Not in trumpet blast. But with quiet inevitability.

He became her messenger, her hand, her confidence. And in time, he was named what only few had ever been:
The Highest Angel.

He does not wear the title. He is it.

And to this day, when the threads grow thin and fate trembles, we do not ask Fionil what shall be done.
We ask Destiny:

“Where does the thread lead, Brother?”
And he, always with that glimmer behind his mask, replies,
“Where it always was meant to go. Let me show you.”

In those early eons, he and I would often walk through the mortal world — Fiondell — talking endlessly about probability, chaos, dreams, and the illusion of certainty. Though we both knew all that was, his joy was in not knowing what would come next.

That was always the difference between us.

I seek understanding. I archive, I observe, I collect what is and preserve it. But Destiny? He seeks the unknown. Not blindly, not foolishly — but deliberately. Like an artist who sketches in the dark just to see what shapes his hand will find.

I remember once we stood on a cliff overlooking a city that had not yet been built. The wind carried sand, and the sun burned crimson behind clouds that would become myths. He turned to me and said:

“Isn’t it lovely? All these paths… none of them walked yet. They scream with possibility. Do you hear it too, brother?”

I did not. But I listened.

He listened to dreams — not just the dreams mortals spoke of, but the ones they never voiced. The ones buried so deep even their souls forgot them. He said they were the truest maps of fate.

Sometimes, he would stop mid-step and stare into nothing, eyes distant behind his mask.

“Someone has chosen to stay instead of go,” he murmured once.
“A shepherd, who should have left his village. Because he stayed, a girl will live. She’ll fall in love with a thief. The thief will become a king. And because of that king… a war will never start.”

“How far do you follow the thread?” I asked him.

“Far enough to know when to let go,” he said, smiling softly. “Fate is not chains, Known. It’s wind. You don’t pull wind… you guide it.”

There was poetry in his madness.

He would bless farmers with unlikely rains, place misfolded letters just where they needed to be, or delay a cartwheel breaking by exactly two minutes, giving a rider just enough time to meet the healer who would later save a nation. None of it felt grand. That was the point.

He loathed grand displays.

“If I have to shout, then I’ve already failed,” he once told a mortal priest who begged him for prophecy.
“Real fate whispers. Loud voices are just thunder chasing rain.”

And yet — when he had to speak loud, when fate strained and chaos pressed too hard — he did not hesitate.

In the fourth age, when a crack formed in the Loomhall — an echo of a future never meant to exist — he stepped into the fracture alone. Not even Fionil knew what would happen. I waited outside the breach for what felt like years. When he returned, he looked older — as if he had worn time like a cloak and found it heavy. He said only:

“I burned the unthreaded path. It would have made gods scream.”

No one asked more. Not even me.

The other angels grew to respect him, but also… to fear him. Not for his power — though his power was beyond most of us — but for what he represented.

He knew what we hid.

He knew Filla’s unspoken envy.
He knew Willia’s ancient regret.
He knew even Clock, eternal and still, once wished to turn back one second — and regretted that wish every day since.

He never judged.

That made it worse.

We all have roles. We are what we are. But Destiny, perhaps more than any of us, chose to be what he is. And in choosing, he made others question themselves.

The gods still adore him — and they should. He moves with grace, speaks with elegance, and never wastes a word. But behind their praise is a quiet awe. They do not control him. Not even Fionil truly does. She guides, yes. But he… he navigates.

He is not just the weaver.
He is the pattern within the weave.

And when I look back at those long walks, I realize now — I was not teaching him anything.
He was teaching me what it means to know something worth not knowing.

~ Known, Angel of Knowledge
